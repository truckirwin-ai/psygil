# BYOB: Build Your Own Business

**A repeatable methodology for taking any business — from a flower shop to a SaaS platform — from idea to launch-ready in one intensive engagement.**

*Extracted from the Psygil build session, March 19, 2026. Designed to serve any business type, any industry, any scale.*

---

## What This Is

BYOB is a structured methodology for producing every document, plan, review, and artifact a new business needs before opening its doors or shipping its product. It compresses months of typical business preparation into one intensive working session by using AI to simulate the expertise that founders would normally need to hire individually: attorneys, accountants, industry consultants, designers, marketers, and operations specialists.

This is not a pitch deck generator. This is the full set of artifacts that banks, investors, landlords, partners, attorneys, and you yourself need to evaluate, fund, and launch the business.

It works for a solo founder building a software product. It works equally well for a couple opening a restaurant, a contractor starting a construction company, a designer launching a studio, or a nurse practitioner opening a private practice.

## What the Founder Brings

BYOB doesn't replace the founder's domain expertise. It accelerates everything around it. The founder needs to bring some minimum foundation:

**Required (any business type):**
- A clear problem or opportunity ("people in this town have nowhere to get good coffee," "forensic psychologists spend 8 hours on documentation that should take 2," "nobody offers residential electrical service on weekends")
- Knowledge of the customer (who they are, where they are, what they currently do)
- Some awareness of the competition (who else serves this market, even roughly)
- A sense of the economics (how money flows — what you charge, what it costs, what's left)
- Willingness to make decisions fast

**Not required:**
- A business plan (that's what we build)
- Legal documents (that's what we draft)
- A marketing strategy (that's what we develop)
- Financial projections (that's what we model)
- A brand or visual identity (that's what we create)

## The 10 Stages

### Stage 1: Deep Read + Honest Assessment

**What happens:** Understand everything the founder already knows — their market, their craft, their competitive landscape, their financial situation, their constraints. Then deliver an honest assessment. Not cheerleading. Assessment. Where are the strengths, where are the gaps, where are the risks they haven't seen.

**The questions (any business):**
- Is the problem real? Can you see people paying to solve it?
- Is the market big enough to sustain this business? (A town of 3,000 may not support a specialty bakery. A neighborhood with 6 existing salons may not need a 7th.)
- What do you know about your customers that your competitors don't?
- What are the regulatory, licensing, or compliance requirements you may not have considered?
- What's the realistic startup cost, and do you have access to it?
- What's your personal financial runway? How long can you survive before the business sustains you?

**Examples across business types:**

| Business | What the assessment reveals |
|----------|-----------------------------|
| Flower shop | The farmer's market 2 miles away sells cut flowers cheaper. Your differentiation is arrangements and delivery — are you pricing delivery to cover it? |
| Restaurant | Your target neighborhood has foot traffic weekdays but dies on weekends. Your lease requires 7-day operation. The economics don't work without weekend revenue. |
| Electrical contractor | You're licensed residential but the margin is in commercial. Getting a commercial license requires 4,000 additional hours. Factor that timeline in. |
| SaaS product | Your architecture handles 100 users but you're projecting 10,000 in Year 2. The database choice doesn't scale. Decide now or pay 10x to rebuild later. |
| Therapy practice | Your state requires supervision for the first 2 years post-licensure. You can't bill insurance directly until supervised hours are complete. The financial model needs a 2-year ramp. |

**Deliverable:** Honest assessment document — strengths, gaps, risks, and recommendations.

---

### Stage 2: Rapid Decision Loop

**What happens:** Present every major business decision with clear options and trade-offs. The founder decides. Each decision gets implemented immediately into the living documents. No decision sits undecided.

**Universal decisions every business faces:**

| Decision | A restaurant asks... | A SaaS founder asks... | An electrician asks... |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Location/distribution | Lease downtown or strip mall? | Electron desktop or web app? | Home office or commercial space? |
| Pricing | Prix fixe or a la carte? | Monthly subscription or per-use? | Hourly or flat-rate per job? |
| Staffing | Hire sous chef now or start solo? | Hire engineer or use AI tools? | Hire an apprentice or subcontract? |
| Legal structure | LLC or S-corp? | Delaware C-corp or LLC? | Sole prop or LLC? |
| Funding | SBA loan, savings, or investor? | SBIR grant, bootstrap, or seed round? | Equipment financing or lease? |
| Brand | Upscale or casual? | Enterprise or developer-first? | Residential or commercial brand? |
| Scope | Full menu or focused concept? | Full feature set or narrow MVP? | All electrical or specialize in panels? |

**Deliverable:** Decision log with rationale preserved. All downstream documents updated.

---

### Stage 3: Core Principle Injection

**What happens:** The founder articulates the non-negotiable values the business must embody. Not a mission statement — an operational principle that shapes every decision.

**This is the founder's soul speaking.** It can't be templated. It can't be generated. It can only be surfaced through conversation and then enforced architecturally.

**Examples across business types:**

| Business | Principle | How it shapes everything |
|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| Psygil (SaaS) | "THE DOCTOR ALWAYS DIAGNOSES. NEVER THE AI." | Restructured agent design, gate UX, audit trail, legal positioning, FDA classification, marketing language |
| Restaurant | "Every plate leaves the kitchen as if the chef's mother is eating it." | Determines portion size, plating standards, ingredient sourcing, hiring criteria for cooks, pricing (can't cut corners to hit a price point) |
| Flower shop | "We never sell a flower we wouldn't put in our own home." | Drives supplier relationships, freshness standards, refund policy, and eliminates the cheap-bouquet market segment |
| Electrical contractor | "We fix it right or we don't fix it." | Means no shortcuts on code compliance, longer job times but zero callbacks, premium pricing justified by warranty, and turning down jobs where the client wants cheap over correct |
| Therapy practice | "The client's safety comes before the practice's revenue." | Means referring out when a client needs a specialist, not overbooking to hit revenue targets, maintaining pro bono slots, and documenting clinical decisions transparently |

**Deliverable:** Principle documented and traced through every affected business decision, policy, and process.

### Stage 4: Domain Expert Panel

**What happens:** Simulate a panel of 5 people who know the industry from different perspectives. Not all customers — include suppliers, regulators, employees, and competitors.

**The panel finds what the founder missed.** The founder knows the business from inside. The panel reveals what it looks like from outside — from the customer who walks in, the inspector who audits, the employee who quits, and the competitor who undercuts.

**Example panels by business type:**

**Restaurant:**
1. A food critic who eats at 200 restaurants a year (what makes a place memorable vs. forgettable)
2. A restaurant general manager with 15 years experience (staffing, inventory, and the things that kill restaurants)
3. A health inspector (the violations they see every day that owners don't realize are violations)
4. A regular customer in the target neighborhood (what they actually want, not what the owner thinks they want)
5. A failed restaurant owner (what went wrong and how it could have been prevented)

**Trades/Contractor:**
1. A general contractor who subcontracts electrical work (what makes them choose one sub over another)
2. A building inspector (the code violations they flag most often)
3. A homeowner who recently had electrical work done (what the experience was like, what they'd change)
4. An insurance adjuster (what claims they see from electrical work gone wrong)
5. A competing electrician (what they're charging, how they're marketing, where they're losing bids)

**Deliverable:** Domain Expert Panel Review with specific concerns, opportunities, and recommendations from each perspective.

---

### Stage 5: Legal & Regulatory Panel

**What happens:** Simulate attorneys and regulatory specialists relevant to the business type. Every business has legal requirements — licenses, permits, contracts, insurance, employment law, tax obligations. Most founders discover these after they've already spent money.

**Universal legal questions every business faces:**
- What licenses and permits do you need? (Business license, professional license, health permit, building permit, signage permit, liquor license, zoning approval)
- What insurance is required? (General liability, professional liability, workers' comp, commercial auto, property, cyber liability)
- What contracts do you need? (Lease, vendor agreements, employment agreements, customer terms, partnership agreements)
- What employment law applies? (Minimum wage, overtime, tip credit, independent contractor classification, anti-discrimination, workers' comp)
- What tax obligations exist? (Sales tax, payroll tax, estimated quarterly, business property tax, use tax)
- What industry-specific regulations apply? (Health codes, OSHA, HIPAA, building codes, environmental, ADA compliance)

**State-specific matters enormously.** Colorado employment law differs from Texas. California health department requirements differ from Florida. The legal panel must be tuned to the founder's jurisdiction.

**Deliverable:** Legal & Regulatory Review with specific requirements, estimated costs, timeline, and a prioritized checklist.

---

### Stage 6: Executive / Operations Review

**What happens:** Simulate a seasoned business operator and a banker/investor who together stress-test the operational readiness and financial viability of the business.

**The operator asks:**
- What's your day-one operations plan? (Who opens? Who closes? What if someone calls in sick?)
- What systems do you need? (POS, scheduling, inventory, accounting, CRM, booking)
- How do you handle a bad day? (Equipment breaks, supplier delivers late, key employee quits, weather kills foot traffic)
- What's your support model? (How do customers reach you with problems? What's your response time?)
- What happens when you're at capacity? (Waitlist? Second location? Raise prices? Hire?)

**The banker/investor asks:**
- Where does every dollar go? (Startup costs itemized, monthly burn, break-even timeline)
- What's the revenue model? (Revenue per customer, customers per day/week/month, seasonality)
- What's the cash flow? (When does money come in vs. go out? Can you survive 3 months of below-projection revenue?)
- What's the risk? (What kills this business? Economic downturn, new competitor, regulation change, key person loss)
- What's the exit? (Sell the business? Franchise? Pass to family? Run it forever? How does it end?)

**Deliverable:** Operations & Financial Review — day-one operations plan, systems requirements, financial model, cash flow projection, risk analysis, and go/no-go criteria.

---

### Stage 7: Business Specifications

**What happens:** Produce the artifacts the business needs to operate. The deliverables change based on business type, but the principle is the same: everything an operator needs to open the doors.

**For a physical business (restaurant, retail, service):**
1. Business plan (operations-focused, not investor-pitch-focused)
2. Floor plan / layout with equipment list and costs
3. Vendor list with pricing, terms, and backup suppliers
4. Staffing plan (roles, compensation, scheduling, hiring timeline)
5. Standard operating procedures (opening, closing, inventory, cleaning, emergency)
6. Menu / service list / pricing schedule
7. Customer experience map (from awareness to first visit to repeat)

**For a professional practice (therapy, dental, law, veterinary):**
1. Practice plan (services, pricing, scheduling, capacity)
2. Technology stack (EHR/EMR, billing, scheduling, telehealth)
3. Compliance protocols (HIPAA, state licensing board, malpractice insurance)
4. Patient/client intake workflow and forms
5. Referral network plan
6. Staffing plan (front desk, billing, clinical support)
7. Marketing plan (physician referrals, insurance panels, community presence)

**For a technology product:**
1. Database schema
2. API contracts
3. Agent/service specifications
4. UI wireframes and design system
5. User stories with acceptance criteria
6. Sprint milestone plan
7. Security architecture

**For a trades/contractor business:**
1. Service catalog with pricing (hourly, flat-rate, or bid-based)
2. Estimating process and templates
3. Job workflow (estimate → contract → schedule → execute → inspect → invoice → collect)
4. Equipment inventory and maintenance schedule
5. Licensing and continuing education tracker
6. Insurance matrix (by job type and risk level)
7. Subcontractor agreements

**Deliverable:** Complete specification set for the business type. Everything needed to operate from day one.

---

### Stage 8: Legal Document Drafting

**What happens:** Draft the legal documents the business needs. Not legal advice — working drafts that a local attorney reviews and finalizes. This saves the founder $5K-$50K in legal fees depending on business complexity.

**Universal documents (every business):**
- Operating agreement or bylaws (LLC or corporation structure)
- Standard customer agreement or terms of service
- Privacy policy (if collecting any customer data)
- Employee handbook essentials (at-will employment, anti-harassment, safety)
- Independent contractor agreement (if using subcontractors)
- Non-disclosure agreement template (for vendors, partners, employees)

**Industry-specific documents:**
- Lease review checklist (physical businesses)
- Franchise agreement (if franchising)
- Patient consent forms (healthcare)
- Informed consent for AI assistance (tech products in regulated industries)
- Regulatory compliance protocols (HIPAA, FDA, OSHA, health department)
- Construction contract templates (contractors)

**Deliverable:** Draft legal documents ready for local attorney review. Not final. Not legal advice. But 80% complete, saving thousands in attorney drafting time.

---

### Stage 9: Brand & Visual Identity

**What happens:** Create the visual identity and customer-facing materials the business needs to present itself professionally.

**For a physical business:**
- Logo concept and color palette
- Signage specifications
- Menu or service catalog design
- Business card and stationery design
- Social media profile setup
- Website design (even a simple one-page site)
- Interior design direction (if relevant)

**For a technology product:**
- Design system (colors, typography, spacing, components)
- Working UI prototype with theme support
- Branding guidelines

**For a professional practice:**
- Professional website with provider bios and service descriptions
- Patient/client intake forms (branded)
- Referral materials for partner providers

**Deliverable:** Visual identity package appropriate to the business type. Not a full brand agency engagement — but enough to open the doors looking professional.

---

### Stage 10: Project Management Infrastructure

**What happens:** Build the tracking system that keeps the founder honest about progress, budget, and risks. Whether it's a living HTML dashboard (for a tech product) or a simple spreadsheet with milestones (for a flower shop), the founder needs something that shows them where they are versus where they planned to be.

**Deliverables:**
- Launch timeline with milestones and dependencies
- Budget tracker (planned vs. actual)
- Risk register with mitigation plans
- Task list prioritized by what blocks launch
- Session prompt / working document for future AI sessions

## What Cannot Be Automated

These require the founder's judgment and no AI should pretend otherwise:

- **The founder's decisions.** Options can be presented. The decision is human.
- **Core principles.** These come from the founder's values, not a template.
- **Local relationships.** The real estate agent who knows which landlords negotiate, the supplier who gives credit to new businesses, the banker who understands your industry — these are human relationships.
- **Attorney review.** Legal drafts need a licensed attorney in the founder's jurisdiction.
- **Taste.** Whether it's the restaurant's ambiance, the shop's aesthetic, or the app's UI — the founder's taste drives the brand.
- **Domain expertise.** The chef knows food. The electrician knows wiring. The therapist knows clinical work. BYOB builds the business infrastructure around that expertise. It doesn't replace it.
