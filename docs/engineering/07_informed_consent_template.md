# Informed Consent Template: AI-Assisted Documentation in Forensic and Clinical Psychological Evaluation

**Document Version:** 1.0
**Last Updated:** March 19, 2026
**Compliance Standards:** APA Ethics Code Standard 9.03 (Informed Consent in Assessments)

---

## CRITICAL LEGAL AND ETHICAL NOTICE

This template addresses the requirement that evaluees receive informed consent disclosure when AI-assisted documentation tools are used during psychological evaluations. This template has been designed specifically to ensure compliance with:

- **APA Ethics Code Standard 9.03**: Psychologists inform clients/subjects about the nature and results of psychological assessments
- **Daubert/Frye Defense Strategy**: Language frames the AI tool as a documentation assistant (analogous to dictation software), NOT a clinical decision-making tool
- **State-Specific Requirements**: California AB 3030, Colorado SB 24-205, and emerging state AI transparency laws

## OVERVIEW: WHEN TO USE EACH VERSION

### Version 1: Full Disclosure (RECOMMENDED FOR FORENSIC EVALUATIONS)
- **Use in:** Custody evaluations, forensic risk assessments, competency evaluations, civil/criminal court-ordered evaluations
- **Format:** Standalone disclosure paragraph for the informed consent section of the evaluation report
- **Length:** Approximately 150-200 words
- **Legal Safety:** Highest level of transparency; recommended for any evaluation likely to be challenged in litigation
- **Rationale:** Forensic evaluations carry substantial legal consequences. Full disclosure demonstrates procedural transparency and good-faith effort to ensure evaluee comprehension of evaluation methodology.

### Version 2: Brief Disclosure (FOR CLINICAL EVALUATIONS WITH LOWER LEGAL STAKES)
- **Use in:** Clinical diagnostic evaluations, routine intake assessments, treatment planning evaluations where litigation is unlikely
- **Format:** 2-3 sentence paragraph within informed consent
- **Length:** 50-75 words
- **Legal Safety:** Adequate for most clinical contexts; appropriate when evaluation findings are unlikely to be contested
- **Rationale:** Clinical evaluations with lower litigation risk can use more concise language while maintaining transparency

### Version 3: Verbal Disclosure Script (INITIAL CONSENT CONVERSATION)
- **Use in:** Spoken disclosure before testing begins; should precede all evaluations regardless of type
- **Format:** Conversational language for clinician to use in informed consent discussion
- **Length:** 60-90 seconds of verbal delivery
- **Legal Safety:** Creates documented record (via notes) that consent was discussed before evaluation; demonstrates procedural integrity
- **Rationale:** Verbal discussion allows the evaluee to ask questions in real-time and ensures understanding before any assessment activities begin

# VERSION 1: FULL DISCLOSURE (FORENSIC EVALUATIONS)

## Recommended Placement
Insert this disclosure in the "Procedures and Informed Consent" or "Methodology" section of the evaluation report, immediately following description of assessment instruments and interview procedures.

---

## TEXT: Full Disclosure Version

### AI-Assisted Documentation Tools in This Evaluation

During this evaluation, I used an artificial intelligence-assisted documentation tool called Psygil to assist with the organization and documentation of evaluation materials. It is important that you understand the specific role this tool played in your evaluation.

**What the AI tool did:** The Psygil tool assisted me with organizing your case file materials, summarizing clinical observations I made directly, and drafting sections of this report. The tool functioned as a documentation assistant—similar to speech-to-text dictation software or word processing tools—to organize the information I gathered and create an initial framework for the final report.

**What the AI tool did NOT do:** The Psygil tool made no diagnostic determinations, clinical judgments, or conclusions about you. The tool did not analyze test results, interpret your interview responses, evaluate your behavioral observations, or influence any clinical decision I made. The tool has no access to confidential clinical information beyond what I provided to it for documentation purposes.

**My role:** All clinical observations during the evaluation sessions were conducted directly by me as your evaluating psychologist. All diagnostic decisions, clinical conclusions, and recommendations in this report represent my independent professional judgment based on the evidence gathered. I reviewed and approved every statement in this report before it was completed. No finding or conclusion in this report reflects the AI tool's analysis or recommendation—all substantive clinical content reflects my professional expertise and judgment.

**Analogy:** The role of Psygil in this evaluation is comparable to how a clinician might use Dragon dictation software to dictate initial clinical notes, or how legal counsel uses word processing software to organize case documents. The tool is a clerical and organizational aid; it does not practice psychology or make clinical decisions.

You were not assessed or evaluated by artificial intelligence. You were evaluated by me, a licensed psychologist, using standard psychological assessment procedures. The AI tool's sole role was to assist with documentation of my clinical work.

# VERSION 2: BRIEF DISCLOSURE (CLINICAL EVALUATIONS)

## Recommended Placement
Insert this disclosure in the "Procedures and Informed Consent" section of the evaluation report or intake documentation.

---

## TEXT: Brief Disclosure Version

### Use of AI-Assisted Documentation Tools

This evaluation used Psygil, an AI-assisted documentation tool, to help organize evaluation materials and draft report sections. However, all clinical observations, diagnostic decisions, and conclusions in this report represent my independent professional judgment. The AI tool functioned as a documentation assistant only—similar to word processing software—and made no diagnostic determinations or clinical recommendations.

# VERSION 3: VERBAL DISCLOSURE SCRIPT

## Timing
Deliver this disclosure at the beginning of the evaluation session, during the informed consent discussion, **before any assessment instruments are administered or formal interviewing begins**. This should be a natural part of your standard informed consent process.

---

## TEXT: Verbal Disclosure Script

### What to say to the evaluee:

---

"Before we get started, I want to tell you about a tool I use to help me do my job more efficiently. I use a computer program called Psygil to help me organize information and write up my notes and reports. I want to be completely clear about what this program does and does not do.

The program helps me organize the records I collect from you and other sources—kind of like how someone might use a word processor or dictation software. It helps me draft sections of the report and keep all the information organized. But the program does **not** make any decisions about you. It does **not** decide what your diagnosis is, it does **not** score your tests, and it does **not** tell me what to think about you.

Everything I observe about you—how you respond to questions, how you perform on tests, what you tell me about your history—that's all coming directly from me. All the decisions I make—what your diagnosis is, what my recommendations are—those decisions are **mine alone**, based on my training and professional judgment.

The computer program is just a tool to help me organize and write, the same way I might use a word processor. You are being evaluated by **me**, a licensed psychologist, not by a computer. I'll be the one making all the clinical decisions based on everything I learn about you in this evaluation.

Do you have any questions about how this tool is used in your evaluation?"

---

### Key points to emphasize in response to evaluee questions:

- **"Will the computer make decisions about me?"** No. The computer only organizes information and helps with writing. I make all the decisions.
- **"Can the computer access my personal information?"** Only the information I provide to it for organizing and writing purposes. It's under the same confidentiality protections as the rest of your evaluation.
- **"Is this the standard way evaluations are done?"** I use this tool to help me work more efficiently, similar to how other professionals use technology. But the nature of the evaluation is the same—comprehensive psychological assessment conducted by a licensed psychologist.
- **"Will this affect my evaluation?"** No. Using this documentation tool does not change how I conduct the evaluation or make clinical decisions. Everything is based on my direct observations and professional judgment.

# REPORT-SPECIFIC PLACEMENT GUIDANCE

## For Custody Evaluations
**Placement:** Include in the "Procedures and Informed Consent" section, immediately after describing assessment methods and time spent with evaluees.

**Rationale:** Custody evaluations are frequently challenged in litigation. Full disclosure demonstrates procedural transparency and creates a clear record of informed consent before testing began.

**Recommended Version:** Version 1 (Full Disclosure)

---

## For Risk Assessment Evaluations
**Placement:** Include in the "Methodology" or "Evaluation Procedures" section, near any disclaimer about the limitations and uses of the risk assessment.

**Rationale:** Risk assessment reports are often used in civil commitment, parole board, and involuntary hospitalization proceedings. Transparency about documentation methodology prevents future challenges to the assessment's validity.

**Recommended Version:** Version 1 (Full Disclosure)

---

## For Competency/Capacity Evaluations
**Placement:** Include in the "Procedures" section, before describing the specific competency domains assessed.

**Rationale:** Competency evaluations have direct legal consequences. Clear disclosure about the evaluation methodology supports the court's understanding of how the evaluation was conducted.

**Recommended Version:** Version 1 (Full Disclosure)

---

## For Clinical Diagnostic Evaluations (Non-Forensic)
**Placement:** Include in the "Assessment Procedures" or "Informed Consent" section of the report.

**Rationale:** Clinical evaluations typically have lower litigation risk. Brief disclosure is appropriate and sufficient.

**Recommended Version:** Version 2 (Brief Disclosure)

---

## For Clinical Treatment Planning Evaluations
**Placement:** Include in intake documentation or in a separate informed consent form reviewed at the first session.

**Rationale:** Treatment planning evaluations focus on treatment recommendations. Brief disclosure is appropriate; emphasis can be placed on how findings will guide treatment.

**Recommended Version:** Version 2 (Brief Disclosure)

# STATE-SPECIFIC LEGAL CONSIDERATIONS

## California (AB 3030)
**Requirement:** California Assembly Bill 3030 (effective 2025) requires disclosure when "automated decision systems" are used in clinical decision-making with patients.

**Application to Psygil:**
- Psygil should NOT be characterized as a "decision system" in California disclosures
- Use the language: "documentation tool" and "clerical assistant" exclusively
- Emphasize that the tool makes zero clinical decisions
- Consider adding: "This tool is not a clinical decision support system; it is a documentation and organization aid only"

**Recommended Language Adjustment for California Evaluations:**
> "I want to clarify that Psygil is a documentation and clerical organization tool, not a clinical decision support system. It has no role in my diagnostic decision-making or clinical judgment, which are conducted entirely independently."

---

## Colorado (SB 24-205)
**Requirement:** Colorado Senate Bill 24-205 requires transparency regarding AI use in healthcare and mental health services.

**Application to Psygil:**
- Clear identification that AI was used for documentation purposes only
- Explicit statement that no clinical decisions were made by the AI
- Opportunity for the evaluee to ask questions about the AI use
- Documentation that consent was obtained

**Recommended Language Adjustment for Colorado Evaluations:**
> "I'm using artificial intelligence as a documentation tool in this evaluation, similar to how healthcare providers use electronic health record systems. The AI assists with organizing and documenting information only; all clinical judgments are mine alone."

---

## Other State Considerations
**General guidance for states without specific AI laws:**
- Use the full disclosure version when evaluation findings are likely to be litigated
- Use the brief disclosure version for routine clinical evaluations
- Always document in your clinical notes that AI documentation tool use was disclosed
- Consider consulting your state psychology licensing board's guidance on AI use in clinical practice

# THE DAUBERT/FRYE FIREWALL: CRITICAL LANGUAGE DISTINCTIONS

## Purpose
The Daubert and Frye standards govern the admissibility of expert testimony and the reliability of expert methodologies. A key vulnerability in expert testimony is whether the expert's methodology was influenced by external factors or tools that lack scientific validation.

If an evaluation is characterized as having been "assisted" or "influenced" by an AI system, opposing counsel may challenge:
1. Whether the AI's outputs were scientifically validated
2. Whether the AI could have introduced bias
3. Whether the expert was relying on the AI's analysis rather than independent judgment
4. Whether the evaluation methodology meets Daubert/Frye standards

## Solution: Precise Language Choices

The following language distinctions create a legal and methodological "firewall" that protects the validity of your evaluation:

---

### LANGUAGE TO ALWAYS USE (SAFE)

| Function | Safe Language | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Record Organization** | "organized the evaluation materials" | Clerical function; no clinical component |
| | "compiled case file information" | Documentation task; psychologist remains decision-maker |
| | "systematized records from multiple sources" | Administrative function parallels word processing |
| **Initial Drafting** | "drafted sections of the report" | Clerical drafting; psychologist reviews and approves all content |
| | "created an initial framework" | Organizational aid; final content is psychologist's work product |
| | "assisted with documentation" | Analogous to dictation software or word processors |
| **Tool Function** | "documentation tool" | Clerical function only |
| | "clerical assistant" | Non-clinical support |
| | "organizational aid" | Analogous to filing systems or spreadsheet software |
| | "word processing assistance" | Clear analogy to standard office tools |
| **Tool Limitation** | "made no clinical decisions" | Establishes clear boundary |
| | "conducted no assessment activities" | Clarifies tool was not used to assess evaluee |
| | "interpreted no test results" | Psychologist retains this core function |
| | "influenced no diagnostic determinations" | Psychologist's independence is explicit |
| **Psychologist's Role** | "I reviewed and approved all content" | Psychologist is the decision-maker |
| | "My independent professional judgment" | Emphasizes psychologist's role as expert |
| | "All diagnostic decisions reflect my clinical expertise" | Clear ownership of clinical determinations |
| | "All clinical observations were made directly by me" | Psychologist conducted the assessment |

---

### LANGUAGE TO NEVER USE (UNSAFE)

| Unsafe Language | Problem | Better Alternative |
|-----------------|---------|-------------------|
| "AI-assisted diagnosis" | Suggests AI participated in diagnostic decision | "AI-assisted documentation; diagnosis made by me" |
| "AI analysis of findings" | Suggests AI analyzed clinical data | "AI organized materials; I analyzed findings" |
| "AI contributed to conclusions" | Suggests AI influenced clinical judgment | "AI drafted sections; I wrote all conclusions" |
| "AI-supported clinical decision" | Suggests AI supported clinical reasoning | "AI helped organize records; I made independent decisions" |
| "AI clinical decision support" | Explicitly names it as decision support system | "AI clerical support for documentation" |
| "AI influenced my opinion" | Admits external influence on expert judgment | "My opinion reflects only my clinical expertise and judgment" |
| "AI helped me evaluate" | Suggests AI participated in evaluation | "I conducted the evaluation; AI helped with documentation" |
| "AI recommended..." | Suggests AI made recommendations | "I recommended..." |
| "AI determined..." | Suggests AI made clinical determination | "I determined..." |
| "AI-enhanced evaluation" | Suggests AI improved or modified evaluation | "Evaluation with AI-assisted documentation" |

---

## Implementation

Before finalizing any disclosure language:

1. **Read aloud:** Does it clearly distinguish between documentation assistance and clinical decision-making?
2. **Hypothetical cross-examination:** Could an attorney argue the AI influenced your clinical judgment based on this language?
3. **Conservative revision:** When in doubt, choose the safer language option
4. **Consult your attorney:** Run final language by your legal counsel before use

# ETHICAL BEST PRACTICES

## Before Each Evaluation

- [ ] Ensure your professional liability insurance covers AI-assisted documentation tools (contact your carrier)
- [ ] Confirm your state licensing board has no contraindications to AI documentation tool use
- [ ] Review your employment contract or practice agreement (if applicable) for any restrictions on technology use
- [ ] Ensure you understand Psygil's data security and privacy practices
- [ ] Confirm that Psygil's output aligns with your professional standards and your clinical judgment

## During Informed Consent

- [ ] Provide disclosure using the language appropriate for your evaluation type
- [ ] Use clear, non-technical language the evaluee can understand
- [ ] Allow time for questions and ensure genuine understanding
- [ ] Document that disclosure occurred and that consent was obtained
- [ ] Be prepared to explain the analogy (word processor, dictation software) if asked

## During the Evaluation

- [ ] Conduct all clinical observations and interviews yourself
- [ ] Make all diagnostic and clinical decisions independently
- [ ] Do not defer to or accept the AI tool's suggested language as your final determination
- [ ] Review all Psygil output carefully before including it in your report
- [ ] Modify or reject any Psygil language that does not accurately reflect your clinical judgment

## After the Evaluation

- [ ] Review the finalized report to confirm all clinical content reflects your judgment
- [ ] Ensure disclosure language appears in the final report as planned
- [ ] Maintain documentation (clinical notes) showing when disclosure occurred
- [ ] Be prepared to explain your methodology if the evaluation is later challenged

# FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS FOR EVALUEES (Optional Handout)

You may wish to provide this FAQ to evaluees who receive disclosure:

---

## Q: What does it mean that an AI tool was used?
**A:** An artificial intelligence tool was used to help organize information from your evaluation and assist with writing sections of the report. This is similar to how a clinician might use word processing software or dictation tools. The AI tool is a clerical aid—it helped with organization and documentation, not with the clinical evaluation itself.

## Q: Did a computer evaluate me instead of a psychologist?
**A:** No. You were evaluated by a licensed psychologist (me). The AI tool only helped with organizing information and writing. The psychologist—not the computer—observed your responses, conducted interviews, made clinical judgments, and wrote the conclusions.

## Q: Can the computer see my personal information?
**A:** The AI tool only sees information the psychologist provided to it for the purpose of organizing and writing the report. It's subject to the same privacy protections as the rest of your evaluation. The tool cannot access other personal information, medical records, or files outside the evaluation.

## Q: Does using this tool affect how good my evaluation is?
**A:** No. Using an AI documentation tool does not change how thoroughly the psychologist conducts the evaluation, how carefully observations are made, or how clinical decisions are reached. The evaluation quality is determined by the psychologist's training, experience, and professional judgment—the same whether or not documentation tools are used.

## Q: What decisions did the AI tool make?
**A:** The AI tool made no decisions. It did not decide what your diagnosis is, did not interpret test results, did not make recommendations, and did not draw any clinical conclusions. The psychologist made all of those decisions independently, based on direct observation and professional expertise.

## Q: Why not just tell me the psychologist was using software?
**A:** Because it's important to be clear and specific about how the evaluation process works. Telling you the truth—that an AI tool assisted with documentation—ensures you understand the evaluation methodology and allows you to ask questions if you have concerns.
